2. Our two roles: Data Processor and Data Fiduciary ↑ top

Under the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 ("DPDP Act"), the person or organisation that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data is the Data Fiduciary. A Data Processor processes personal data solely on the documented instructions of the Data Fiduciary. The individual to whom the personal data relates is the Data Principal.

6. When we process without consent (legitimate uses) ↑ top

Section 7 of the DPDP Act allows certain processing without separate consent. The following legitimate uses may apply:

  • Data you give us voluntarily for a specific purpose, where you have not indicated that you object - for example, when you type your details into our enquiry form to ask us for a demo.
  • Employment purposes - including processing to protect us from loss or liability, and processing related to services or benefits we provide to our own employees.
  • Compliance with law - where an Indian law, a court order or a judgment requires us to disclose or retain data.
  • Medical emergency - to respond to a threat to the life or immediate health of any person, or during an epidemic, outbreak or other threat to public health, or a disaster or breakdown of public order. This one matters mainly for healthcare providers using our products: a healthcare provider does not have to stop to take consent before treating an unconscious patient. Where our software is used in that situation, we process on the healthcare provider's instruction under the same legitimate use.

Even where processing is permitted without consent, we process personal data only for the applicable lawful purpose, implement appropriate safeguards, and respect your rights under applicable law.

11.2 Patient data where the healthcare provider is the Data Fiduciary

The healthcare provider sets the retention rules in the DPA. The following principles apply:

  • Healthcare records cannot always be deleted immediately upon request where retention is required by applicable law or professional obligations. Health records must be retained for the periods required by applicable healthcare provider's record-keeping requirements - including the retention requirements that apply under the Telemedicine Practice Guidelines, 2020 for teleconsultation records, and any longer period the healthcare provider is required or advised to observe. A request to erase a healthcare provider's record will therefore usually be refused for as long as the record must be kept.
  • Deleted records may remain subject to retention or legal hold requirements and will not be permanently removed until those obligations have expired.

14. Data breach notification ↑ top

A "personal data breach" means any unauthorised processing, or accidental disclosure, acquisition, sharing, use, alteration, destruction or loss of access, that compromises the confidentiality, integrity or availability of personal data. There is no severity threshold in the Act - a breach is reportable whether or not it looks serious.

Where we are the Data Fiduciary (website, enquiries, hiring), on becoming aware of a breach we will:

  • tell each affected Data Principal without delay, through the channel they are registered with us on, in clear plain language - describing the nature and extent of the breach, when and where it happened, its likely consequences, what we are doing about it, what you can do to protect yourself, and who to contact;
  • tell the Data Protection Board of India without delay with the facts we have at that point; and
  • give the Board a detailed report within 72 hours of becoming aware (or such longer period as the Board allows on request), covering the events and circumstances leading to the breach, the mitigation measures taken, our findings on who caused it, the remedial steps to prevent recurrence, and a report on the notices given to Data Principals.

Where we are the Data Processor (patient data), the duty to notify the Board and the patients sits with the healthcare provider - and their clock is the same 72 hours. So we notify the affected healthcare provider without delay and in any event within 48 hours , with the facts we have, and we help them prepare their notifications.

15. Children and persons with disability ↑ top

A "child" under the DPDP Act is anyone under 18.

This website is not aimed at children. We do not knowingly collect personal data of anyone under 18 through this website or our enquiry forms. We do not track, behaviourally monitor, or serve targeted advertising to children.

15.1 Patient data relating to a child

The general rule is that a Data Fiduciary must obtain verifiable consent from a parent or lawful guardian before processing a child's personal data, and must not track, behaviourally monitor or target advertising at children.

There is an important exception that applies to our customers. Under Rule 12 and the Fourth Schedule of the DPDP Rules, 2025, a healthcare provider's establishment, mental health establishment, or a healthcare or allied healthcare professional is exempt from those requirements to the extent that the processing is restricted to providing health services to the child. The exemption is narrow: it is limited to that purpose, it does not permit any secondary use, and every other obligation under the Act - notice, security, breach reporting, retention limits - continues to apply in full.

Whether a given healthcare provider qualifies, and how far the exemption stretches over a particular activity, is the healthcare provider's assessment to make and document as Data Fiduciary. Nevic Labs does not make that call. Where the healthcare provider tells us that parental or guardian consent is required, our software supports recording who gave it and when.

Note that a WhatsApp reminder sent to a parent about a child's appointment is likely to sit inside "providing health services". A marketing message about a healthcare provider offer is not. healthcare providers should not assume the exemption covers engagement they would find hard to describe as care.

15.2 Persons with disability

Where a Data Principal is a person with a disability who has a lawful guardian, verifiable consent must come from that guardian. The guardian's status must be confirmed by reference to a court order, an authority designated under the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016, or a Local Level Committee under the National Trust Act, 1999. That verification is the healthcare provider's responsibility as Data Fiduciary, not ours.

16. Where data is stored ↑ top

Application data for Nevic Pulse and Nevic Connect is hosted on AWS in the Mumbai region (ap-south-1), within India.

Some of our service providers are based outside India or may process data outside India - most notably Meta, in relation to WhatsApp message traffic.

The DPDP Act does not require data localisation as a default. It works the other way round: transfer is permitted unless the Central Government restricts a particular country by notification, and the Government may also specify requirements for transfer by general or special order. Localisation obligations can apply to entities notified as Significant Data Fiduciaries. Nevic Labs has not been notified as a Significant Data Fiduciary - that is a Government designation, not something we self-assess or opt into.

Any sector-specific restriction that binds the healthcare provider continues to apply on top of this, and the healthcare provider should satisfy itself about that independently.

18. Where we are in the DPDP transition ↑ top

The DPDP Act and Rules are being brought into force in stages. The Data Protection Board of India was established in November 2025 and can receive complaints. The substantive operational obligations - notice, consent, security safeguards, breach reporting, retention, children's data, cross-border transfer - take full effect in May 2027. Consent Manager registration opens before that.

Our first pilot deployment is expected in September 2026, which falls inside this transition window. We are building to the Rules as notified rather than waiting for the deadline, because retrofitting consent and audit design into a live healthcare provider's system is worse than doing it once, properly, up front. Where a specific mechanism is not yet live in our product, we say so in this policy rather than describing an intention as if it were a fact.
